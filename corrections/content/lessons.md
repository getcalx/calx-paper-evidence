## 2026-03-11 — Category-defining posts need the founder in them
**Observation:** Reviewer-GM2 reviewed the Vocabulary Gap LinkedIn post (8.5/10) and flagged that the post is analytically tight but Spencer is absent from it. Every line could be written by a smart analyst. The fix: one line of lived experience ("I've watched this happen to people I know") transforms it from observation to founder story. The post's strategy came from 7 Prism thinkers who shaped the *what*. It took a practitioner to catch that the *who* was missing.
**Takeaway:** Category-defining content names problems. Founder-brand content puts a person behind the naming. The best posts do both. Always include at least one line that could only come from Spencer — a personal observation, a specific experience, a moment of recognition. Without it, the post is a think-piece. With it, it's a founder story.

## 2026-03-11 — One post is not a strategy; a publishing cadence is
**Observation:** Reviewer-GM2 emphasized that the Vocabulary Gap post is "post 1 of 50, not a standalone." The post-publish strategy matters as much as the post: daily follow-up examples, comment engagement as live product demos, Dani commenting from the recruiter side, screenshot best comments for repost. Category ownership comes from repetition — saying the same thing 50 different ways until people associate your name with the term.
**Takeaway:** For category-defining content, plan the cadence before publishing the first post. Each post should feed the next. Comments generate follow-up content. Advisor engagement (Dani) creates social proof. A content series compounds; a one-off post decays.
