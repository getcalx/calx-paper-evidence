## 2026-03-11 — ACE is Agentic Context Engineering, not Adaptive Correction and Evaluation
**Correction:** Spencer corrected the ACE acronym and framing. ACE = Agentic Context Engineering, from "Evolving Contexts for Self-Improving Language Models." Spencer built the reinforcement loop into the agent orchestration layer BEFORE finding the paper. The research validated what he'd already productized. Most people are still trying to understand ACE. Spencer shipped it.
**Context:** Prism's sub-agents referenced ACE as "Adaptive Correction and Evaluation" and framed it as something Spencer adopted from research. Both were wrong. The framing matters because "adopted a methodology" is a weaker founder signal than "built it first, found the validation after."
**Takeaway:** ACE = Agentic Context Engineering. Spencer productized it before finding the paper. Never frame it as adopted — frame it as validated. This is a founder execution signal, not a methodology reference.

## 2026-03-11 — Shiller can evolve positions across evaluations
**Observation:** Shiller dissented on canvas deferral in the PRD V3 eval (8/10) but updated to "build minimum shareable artifact, not full canvas" in the strategy eval (10/10). The strategy doc gave him enough context to refine his position.
**Takeaway:** When running Prism on related topics, reference prior evaluations explicitly. Thinkers should be allowed to evolve positions when new evidence is presented. Track position changes — they're signal.

## 2026-03-11 — Independent convergence from orthogonal frameworks is the highest-value finding
**Observation:** Reviewer-BH2 (choice architecture) and Reviewer-BH4 (Hook Model) converged independently on "don't gate memory, gate proactivity" for the free/paid split. They came from completely different frameworks and landed on the same structural conclusion.
**Takeaway:** In the synthesis, flag independent convergence from orthogonal frameworks explicitly. It's worth more than any single thinker's recommendation because it's less likely to be framework-specific bias.

## 2026-03-11 — A split with upstream consensus is more useful than a resolution
**Observation:** The 4/4 split on career fluency vs. career intelligence was informative precisely because of the 10/10 consensus underneath it: "name the phenomenon first." The split itself told Spencer the names serve different audiences. Forcing a resolution would have lost that information.
**Takeaway:** Don't always resolve contradictions. Sometimes the split IS the finding. Present the split, identify what's upstream of it, and let Spencer make the judgment call.

## 2026-03-11 — Strategy evals produce stronger consensus than product evals
**Observation:** The strategy eval produced 10/10 consensus on canvas deferral (vs. 8/10 on PRD V3), 10/10 on narrative reordering (new), and escalated free-to-paid from 5/10 to 10/10. Broader strategic context gives thinkers more surface area to agree.
**Takeaway:** Compound decision types (strategy + category + product + fundraising) produce richer Prism output than single-type evaluations. Worth the extra tokens.

## 2026-03-11 — Adding a thinker post-eval reveals blind spots the original panel missed
**Observation:** Reviewer-GM2 was added to the strategy eval after the initial 10-thinker run. His biggest finding — "the founder is invisible" — was a blind spot no other thinker surfaced. The original 10 analyzed the strategy's internal logic (narrative sequence, behavioral economics, category design). None asked "who is the distribution mechanism?" because none of them are practitioners who think in terms of founder-as-media-company. The absence of a marketing practitioner lens left a fundamental GTM gap unexamined.
**Takeaway:** When an eval covers GTM, distribution, or marketing strategy, Reviewer-GM2 should be in the initial rotation, not added after. Practitioner lenses catch execution blind spots that framework-heavy thinkers miss. The original panel was strong on *what to build* but blind on *how anyone finds out it exists*.

## 2026-03-11 — Reviewer-GM2 resolves framework tensions through practitioner sequencing
**Observation:** The Reviewer-GM3/Reviewer-ST2 tension on category timing (name now vs. wait for PMF signals) seemed like a genuine contradiction in the 10-thinker eval. Reviewer-GM2 resolved it by distinguishing between "publishing about the phenomenon" and "premature category declaration." Publishing builds audience → audience validates category → category attracts investors. This reframes the tension as a false binary. Neither Reviewer-GM3 nor Reviewer-ST2 had this resolution because Reviewer-GM3 thinks in category design process and Reviewer-ST2 thinks in fundraising discipline — neither thinks in content cadence.
**Takeaway:** Practitioner thinkers can resolve theoretical tensions by introducing execution-level distinctions the framework thinkers can't see. When two thinkers disagree and both seem right, check if a practitioner lens reveals that the disagreement is about sequencing/execution, not strategy.

## 2026-03-11 — Thinkers can review content, not just strategy
**Observation:** Reviewer-GM2 reviewed the Vocabulary Gap LinkedIn post and provided specific, actionable feedback (rhythm, fold point, naming ceremony, founder voice, post-publish cadence strategy). This is a different mode than the strategy eval — applied craft review, not strategic assessment. The feedback was materially different from what the 7 thinkers who contributed to the original draft provided, because those 7 shaped the *strategy* of the post while Reviewer-GM2 evaluated the *execution* as a content practitioner.
**Takeaway:** Prism can operate in "content review" mode, not just "strategic evaluation" mode. For content, practitioners (Reviewer-GM2) evaluate differently than framework thinkers — they catch rhythm, voice, and post-publish tactics that strategic thinkers don't address. Consider routing content drafts through practitioner lenses specifically.
